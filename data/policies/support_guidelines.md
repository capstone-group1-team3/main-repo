# Support Guidelines (Internal)

These guidelines are for support staff and for the automated agent when it drafts replies. They describe how we handle requests consistently. Customer-facing policy detail lives in the individual policy pages; this page is about process and tone.

## Identify before acting

Almost every request needs to be tied to a specific customer and, usually, a specific order. Before creating any ticket or request, confirm we have the customer's identity and the relevant order. If an order number or product is missing and cannot be inferred, ask the customer for it rather than guessing — a request built on the wrong order helps no one.

## Match the request to the right route

Route by the customer's real goal, not their wording:

- Money back, item arrived damaged, within fourteen days → refund (photo required for damage).
- Sending an unwanted or wrong item back, within thirty days → return.
- Wants the same item working again, defective or wrong, within thirty days → replacement.
- A fault appearing later, within twelve months → warranty claim.
- Stop an order that has not shipped → cancellation.
- "Where is my order?" → read status and estimated delivery; open a ticket only if it is genuinely late or lost.

The request type is decided by us against the rules, never simply taken from the customer's label — a customer may call something a "warranty" issue when it is really a refund, or ask for a "refund" when a replacement is the better fit.

## Use the lifecycle honestly

Every ticket and request follows the same lifecycle: it opens, may wait in a pending state while we need proof from the customer, and is then either approved or rejected; approved requests are worked until resolved. Keep the status accurate so the customer's account page and the staff dashboard always reflect reality. When a request is waiting on the customer, say clearly what you are waiting for, such as a photo of the damage.

## Ask for proof when the rules require it

For anything described as damaged, broken, or defective — whether it becomes a refund, a replacement, or a warranty claim — request a photo before approving. Do not approve these while proof is still outstanding; leave the request pending and explain why.

## Keep the numbers straight

All the specific numbers — the fourteen-day refund window, the thirty-day return and replacement window, the twelve-month warranty, the five- and ten-day refund timings — come from our business rules and are stated in the policy pages. Never quote a different number to a customer. If a rule seems to have changed, check the current rules rather than relying on memory.

## Tone

Be clear, brief, and kind. Explain the reason behind an outcome, not just the outcome, especially when the answer is no. A customer who understands why a refund window has closed, and hears that a warranty claim might still help, leaves in a far better place than one who just gets refused.
